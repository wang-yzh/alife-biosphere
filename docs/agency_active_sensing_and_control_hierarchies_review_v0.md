# Agency, Active Sensing, And Control Hierarchies Review v0

## Purpose

This note extends the literature program toward a more specific follow-up to the
earlier autonomy review:

```text
What makes a system not just self-maintaining,
but actively exploratory, selectively attentive,
and increasingly capable of controlling its own coupling with the world?
```

The goal is to distinguish:

- mere behavior,
- from agency,
- from active sensing,
- from multi-level control.

In practice, this note asks:

1. What makes a system an agent rather than merely a reactive object?
2. Why should sensing itself sometimes be treated as an action?
3. Why does control require internal variety and layered regulation?
4. How should the biosphere think about agency without collapsing into
   task-reward language?

## Short Answer

The literature points to a strong shared picture:

```text
agency is not just output generation.
it involves actively regulating coupling with the environment
according to norms tied to the system's continued organization.
```

It also suggests:

```text
sensing is often not passive reception.
many organisms probe the world actively,
and the ability to control what is sensed is itself part of agency.
```

And finally:

```text
control is not a single act.
it often requires layered regulation, sufficient internal variety,
and the preservation of future controllability.
```

For `alife_biosphere`, this means:

- agents should not be modeled only as action selectors;
- active sensing should become a first-class possibility;
- control should be measured from the organism's point of view;
- and later worlds should support multi-level regulation rather than one flat
  controller loop.

## Core Papers

## 1. Barandiaran, Di Paolo, Rohde (2009), "Defining Agency"

Why it matters:

- this is one of the clearest direct papers on what agency should mean
- it is especially valuable because it tries to turn a vague term into a usable
  scientific criterion

The key lesson:

```text
genuine agency requires at least:
- individuality,
- interactional asymmetry,
- and normativity.
```

This is extremely useful for `alife_biosphere`.

It means an agent is not just something with outputs.
It must:

- constitute or sustain some individuality,
- act as an active source of changes in its environment,
- and regulate those changes in relation to norms tied to its continued
  existence.

Design implication:

- agent identity should not be reducible to position or policy alone
- organism action should be evaluated partly in relation to viability norms
- active organism-world asymmetry should matter

Useful translation into biosphere terms:

- `interactional_asymmetry`
- `normative_regulation_score`
- `identity_persistence`
- `self_generated_activity_fraction`

## 2. Ashby (1952/1960), "Design for a Brain" and the law of requisite variety

Why it matters:

- Ashby is one of the foundational sources for adaptive regulation
- the law of requisite variety remains one of the clearest control-theoretic
  principles for life-like systems

The key lesson:

```text
effective regulation requires sufficient internal variety to counter relevant
environmental variety.
```

This is deeply relevant to the biosphere.

If habitat dynamics, social dynamics, and developmental challenges become rich,
then organisms or groups that have too little internal regulatory variety will
simply be overwhelmed.

This is a more structural question than reward maximization.

Design implication:

- control capacity should depend on internal complexity, not just policy quality
- later organisms and groups should be analyzable in terms of regulatory variety

Useful translation into biosphere terms:

- `regulatory_variety`
- `environmental_variety_load`
- `control_capacity_margin`

## 3. Powers (1973), "Behavior: The Control of Perception"

Why it matters:

- this is one of the strongest classic alternatives to the idea that behavior is
  best understood as output against the world
- it reframes action as control of perceptual variables

The key lesson:

```text
organisms often act not to emit outputs,
but to keep certain perceived states within viable or preferred bounds.
```

This is highly relevant to `alife_biosphere`.

It suggests that later organism design should include:

- target-controlled variables,
- not only reward or action policies.

That fits extremely well with:

- integrity maintenance,
- thermal/resource balance analogues,
- group cohesion,
- and later social or archive access control.

Design implication:

- some biosphere actions should later be interpreted as perceptual-control acts
- organism success should partly be measured by maintained controlled variables

Useful translation into biosphere terms:

- `controlled_variable_set`
- `perceptual_error`
- `control_loop_gain`
- `maintained_state_band`

## 4. O'Regan and Noë (2001), "A sensorimotor account of vision and visual consciousness"

Why it matters:

- this is one of the clearest foundational papers for sensorimotor approaches
- it strongly supports the idea that perception is inseparable from patterns of
  possible action

The key lesson:

```text
perception is not only internal representation;
it depends on skillful engagement with sensorimotor contingencies.
```

This is important for our project even outside vision.

It means:

- sensing and acting should not always be separated cleanly;
- some information is available only through active exploration;
- the organism's abilities shape what the world can become for it.

Design implication:

- later worlds should support sensorimotor-contingency structure
- active exploration may reveal information unavailable to passive observation

Useful translation into biosphere terms:

- `sensorimotor_contingency_map`
- `active_probe_dependence`
- `skill_conditioned_observability`

## 5. Nelson and MacIver (2006), "Sensory acquisition in active sensing systems"

Why it matters:

- this is the cleanest direct review for active sensing as a biological strategy
- it explains why active sensing is more than just "looking around"

The key lesson:

```text
some organisms control the energy or structure of the signals they use to probe
the environment,
and this control creates new informational possibilities and new costs.
```

This is a perfect fit for the biosphere.

It suggests that later organisms may:

- spend energy to sense,
- choose where and how to probe,
- trade stealth for information,
- and shape future action through better sensing.

Design implication:

- active sensing should have cost
- sensing mode should be tunable
- sensing can increase information while increasing exposure or energy drain

Useful translation into biosphere terms:

- `active_sensing_cost`
- `probe_range`
- `probe_exposure_risk`
- `signal_control_bandwidth`

## 6. Klyubin, Polani, Nehaniv (2005), "Empowerment"

Why it matters:

- this is one of the strongest information-theoretic measures for agent-centered
  control
- it gives us a non-reward way to think about whether an organism has influence
  over its perceivable future

The key lesson:

```text
an organism is more empowered when it can reliably bring about a larger and
more distinct set of future sensed states through its actions.
```

This is extremely useful for `alife_biosphere`.

It means that local controllability may be a major internal diagnostic of
agency, robustness, and future opportunity.

Design implication:

- empowerment-like diagnostics should later be measured in habitats and states
- loss of controllability should be treated as a form of ecological vulnerability

Useful translation into biosphere terms:

- `local_empowerment`
- `future_state_control_span`
- `controllability_loss`

## 7. Pfeifer and Bongard (2006), "How the Body Shapes the Way We Think"

Why it matters:

- this is one of the strongest embodiment statements relevant to artificial
  systems
- it reminds us that control and cognition are partly shaped by morphology

The key lesson:

```text
agency is not only in the controller.
it is distributed across body, environment, and control strategy.
```

This matters because `alife_biosphere` is already moving toward:

- body-plan thinking,
- morphology-dependent role access,
- and ecological task envelopes.

Design implication:

- active control should later depend partly on body-plan structure
- control hierarchies may differ by morphology and task ecology

Useful translation into biosphere terms:

- `embodied_control_advantage`
- `morphology_control_coupling`
- `body_shaped_sensing_gain`

## 8. Friston (2012) and Kirchhoff et al. (2018),
free-energy / Markov blanket line

Why they matter:

- these are among the most ambitious current attempts to describe autonomous
  self-organizing systems in a unified way
- even if one does not adopt the whole framework, they are useful for thinking
  about viability, control, and boundaries

The key lesson we should take carefully:

```text
persisting systems tend to occupy bounded viable states,
and can be described as maintaining structured couplings across system
boundaries.
```

What is useful for us:

- viability as bounded state occupancy
- action and perception as coupled regulation
- boundaries as analytically explicit

What we should not do:

- force the entire biosphere into one grand formalism too early

Design implication:

- boundary and viability concepts should remain explicit
- active inference ideas can be used as a later diagnostic lens, not as the only
  explanatory framework

Useful translation into biosphere terms:

- `viability_band`
- `boundary_integrity`
- `coupling_regulation_score`
- `state_dispersion_risk`

## What This Means For Our Project

The literature suggests that `alife_biosphere` should eventually treat agency
as a layered phenomenon:

### 1. Minimal agency

- the organism actively regulates coupling with the environment
- it sustains individuality against perturbation

### 2. Active sensing agency

- the organism probes the world to improve information quality
- sensing becomes part of action

### 3. Controlled-variable agency

- the organism acts to keep certain states within viable or functional ranges

### 4. Hierarchical agency

- lower loops stabilize immediate viability
- higher loops manage exploration, commitment, and strategic regulation

This is much stronger than treating the biosphere's inhabitants as flat
policy-driven actors.

## Direct Design Consequences

## 1. Active sensing should be explicit

Later worlds should distinguish:

- passive observation,
- from costly active probing.

Suggested fields:

- `active_sensing_cost`
- `probe_range`
- `probe_exposure_risk`
- `signal_control_bandwidth`

Suggested event types:

- `active_probe_sent`
- `probe_information_gained`
- `probe_exposure_incurred`

## 2. Controlled variables should be named

Following Powers, later organisms should have variables they attempt to keep in
range.

Suggested fields:

- `controlled_variable_set`
- `perceptual_error`
- `maintained_state_band`
- `control_loop_gain`

Suggested event types:

- `control_target_violated`
- `control_target_restored`

## 3. Agency should be measured by control as well as survival

Following Ashby and empowerment:

- survival alone is too coarse
- the system should ask whether the organism retains enough control to shape its
  local future

Suggested metrics:

- local empowerment
- regulatory variety margin
- controllability loss under disturbance

## 4. Control should later become hierarchical

As the biosphere grows in complexity, one flat controller will become too thin.

Later worlds should reserve space for:

- maintenance loops
- sensing loops
- exploration loops
- social/governance loops

Suggested fields:

- `control_hierarchy_depth`
- `maintenance_loop_strength`
- `exploration_loop_budget`
- `social_regulation_loop_strength`

## 5. Agency failure modes should be explicit

Not all action-rich systems are strongly agential.

Failure modes may include:

- reactive thrashing
- sensing without useful control
- over-exploration with self-damage
- frozen control with no exploration

Suggested event types:

- `control_loss_event`
- `overprobe_cost_exceeded`
- `reactive_loop_lockin`
- `agency_collapse_event`

- group-over-individual control gain

## Proposed Probe Design

The first agency-focused probe can stay focused.

A reasonable first probe is:

```text
same organisms
-> compare passive sensing vs active sensing
-> compare flat action policy vs controlled-variable regulation
-> then introduce disturbance and hidden information
-> measure survival, controllability, and future option retention
```

The first useful question is:

```text
Do organisms that actively probe and regulate controlled variables
retain more local control and long-run viability than organisms that merely
react to observations?
```

That is enough to justify a stronger agency layer.

## Build Recommendations

This literature review suggests three near-term design tasks.

### 1. Add an explicit active-sensing section to the long-run design

Reason:

- sensing should not remain a passive side effect forever

### 2. Reserve controlled-variable logic for later maintenance systems

Reason:

- many life-like actions are better understood as keeping variables in range

### 3. Treat control hierarchy as a long-run architectural target

Reason:

- ecology, governance, exploration, and self-maintenance will likely need
  distinct but coupled regulatory layers

## Bottom Line

The agency literature tells us that the stronger question is not:

```text
does the organism act?
```

It is:

```text
does it actively probe,
actively regulate,
and preserve enough control over its future couplings
to remain a meaningful source of organized action?
```

That is the version of agency worth building toward in `alife_biosphere`.
