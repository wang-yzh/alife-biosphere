# Higher-Level Individuality Claim Standard v0

## Purpose

This note defines how `alife_biosphere` should talk about higher-level
individuality without drifting into overclaim.

The project already has:

- group-design documents,
- major-transitions literature reviews,
- group-reproduction and higher-level-selection literature reviews.

What was still missing is a **claim standard**:

```text
When are we allowed to say:
- "there are groups"
- "group-level selection is present"
- "groups have become higher-level individuals"
and when are we not allowed to say those things?
```

This document is meant to prevent the exact kind of self-deception that often
appears in artificial-life projects when grouping, persistence, and novelty are
 visually impressive.

## Short Answer

The literature supports a strict ladder:

```text
co-location
!= cooperation
!= group adaptation
!= group reproduction
!= higher-level individuality
```

For this project, the default policy should be:

```text
Use the weakest defensible claim.
Upgrade the claim only when the stronger one is directly supported by logged
evidence and controls.
```

## 1. Core Literature Behind The Standard

### [West et al. 2015, "Major evolutionary transitions in individuality"](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4547252/)

Main result:

- a major transition in individuality requires more than cooperation;
- mutual dependence, conflict suppression, communication, and division of labor
  matter;
- higher-level units should increasingly behave as Darwinian individuals.

What it implies for us:

- group formation alone is weak evidence;
- stable cooperation alone is still weak evidence;
- we should not claim higher-level individuality without evidence that the
  group is becoming the relevant unit of persistence and reproduction.

### [Shelton and Michod 2020, "Group and individual selection during evolutionary transitions in individuality: meanings and partitions"](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7133510/)

Main result:

- group and individual selection should be separated carefully;
- the key issue is fitness decoupling, not narrative enthusiasm.

What it implies for us:

- if individual-level success inside the group still explains everything, then
  we do not yet have strong higher-level evidence;
- group-level claims require explicit partitioning or counterfactual logic.

### [Michod et al. 2006, "The group covariance effect and fitness trade-offs during evolutionary transitions in individuality"](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/16751277/)

Main result:

- during transitions in individuality, fitness is reorganized from lower-level
  units toward the higher-level unit.

What it implies for us:

- we need evidence that some cost or fitness is being shifted upward;
- if every member remains best understood as optimizing for itself alone, the
  transition claim is too strong.

### [Libby and Rainey 2013, "A conceptual framework for the evolutionary origins of multicellularity"](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/23735467/)

Main result:

- there are intermediate states with only partial Darwinian status;
- higher-level individuality is not all-or-none.

What it implies for us:

- the project should allow intermediate claims;
- we do not need to choose only between:
  - "nothing happened"
  - "a new individual evolved"

### [Maliet, Shelton, and Michod 2015, "A model for the origin of group reproduction during the evolutionary transition to multicellularity"](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4528466/)

Main result:

- group reproduction is both a precondition and an outcome of higher-level
  selection;
- life-cycle organization matters.

What it implies for us:

- explicit group reproduction is a much stronger signal than persistence or
  clustering;
- until group reproduction exists, claims should remain modest.

### [Black, Bourrat, and Rainey 2020, "Ecological scaffolding and the evolution of individuality"](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41559-019-1086-9)

Main result:

- ecological conditions can scaffold Darwinian-like properties at higher levels.

What it implies for us:

- patch structure and bottlenecks can create candidate higher-level units,
  but ecological scaffolding is not yet the same as completed individuality.

### [Schenkel et al. 2026, "Evolutionary transitions and reversions in individuality"](https://academic.oup.com/jeb/article/39/4/423/8443358)

Main result:

- individuality can be gained and lost;
- reversions matter.

What it implies for us:

- any strong individuality claim should be stress-tested against breakdown and
  reversal, not judged from one favorable window.

## 2. Claim Ladder

The biosphere should use the following ladder.

### Level 0. Aggregation / Co-location

Allowed meaning:

- organisms occupy the same patch or remain spatially associated.

Evidence:

- spatial proximity
- repeated co-occupancy
- shared local resource use

Not enough to claim:

- cooperation
- group adaptation
- group selection
- individuality

### Level 1. Functional Grouping

Allowed meaning:

- organisms interact non-randomly in ways that affect one another's outcomes.

Evidence:

- repeated resource sharing
- repeated signal exchange
- lower within-group aggression than between-group aggression
- group persistence longer than chance clusters

Still not enough to claim:

- group-level selection
- group reproduction
- higher-level individuality

### Level 2. Ecologically Meaningful Group

Allowed meaning:

- group structure affects ecological outcomes beyond simple co-location.

Evidence:

- group persistence improves habitat occupancy or survival;
- role asymmetry appears;
- offspring-group investment begins;
- founder groups alter later local outcomes.

Still not enough to claim:

- strong fitness decoupling
- group reproduction
- higher-level individuality

### Level 3. Candidate Group-Level Selection

Allowed meaning:

- group-level traits plausibly affect persistence or success in ways that are
  not reducible to immediate individual clustering alone.

Minimum evidence:

- a group-level trait is measurable;
- that trait predicts persistence or success across groups;
- individual-only summaries are insufficient to explain the effect;
- controls rule out trivial spatial or demographic explanation.

Typical evidence forms:

- group role differentiation predicts survival;
- group-level bottleneck history predicts later persistence;
- group-level interaction structure predicts rescue or colonization.

Still not enough to claim:

- higher-level individuality

Reason:

- group-level selection can exist before a higher-level Darwinian individual is
  established.

### Level 4. Candidate Higher-Level Reproduction

Allowed meaning:

- groups begin to act as reproduction-bearing entities.

Minimum evidence:

- offspring groups exist as descendants of parent groups;
- some group-level traits persist across those group-level descents;
- founder width and bottleneck structure are logged;
- group-level lineage continuity becomes analyzable.

Still not enough to claim:

- completed higher-level individuality

Reason:

- reproduction is necessary, but not sufficient.

### Level 5. Provisional Higher-Level Individuality

Allowed meaning:

- the group has enough persistence, reproduction, dependence, and conflict
  suppression that it is scientifically fair to discuss it as an incipient
  higher-level individual.

Minimum evidence bundle:

1. **group reproduction**
   - groups produce descendant groups
2. **fitness decoupling evidence**
   - group success cannot be reduced to member success alone
3. **dependence**
   - members or roles become less viable in isolation
4. **conflict suppression**
   - within-group conflict is measurably constrained
5. **role asymmetry or division of labor**
   - some differentiated contribution exists
6. **persistence across disturbance**
   - the group-level organization survives at least some stress or shows
     interpretable recovery

Even here, the word should be used carefully:

```text
provisional
incipient
candidate higher-level individuality
```

### Level 6. Strong Higher-Level Individuality Claim

This project should treat this level as rare and difficult.

Required evidence:

- repeated descendant groups over multiple generations;
- stable group-level lineages;
- robust fitness decoupling;
- clear role interdependence;
- group-level failure and recovery modes distinct from member-level stories;
- reversibility tested and not immediately collapsing the organization;
- controls showing the result is not an artifact of one graph or one shock
  schedule.

This level should not be claimed lightly.

## 3. Evidence Types We Accept

### Acceptable evidence

- event logs showing repeated group-founded descendants
- counterfactual or partition-style analyses of group vs member contribution
- disturbance probes showing group-level persistence differences
- role-specific dependence or damage concentration
- explicit founder and bottleneck history

### Weak evidence

- stable clusters
- high co-occupancy
- pretty spatial structures
- high signaling frequency
- resource pooling without reproduction consequences
- single-seed anecdotes

These may justify Level 1 or Level 2 claims, but not higher.

## 4. Required Controls Before Stronger Claims

Before any claim at Level 3 or above, at least one matched control should exist
for each relevant mechanism.

Minimum controls:

- same habitat without group bottleneck
- same habitat with random rather than structured grouping
- same graph with grouping disabled
- same disturbance treatment without group inheritance

Before any Level 5 or Level 6 claim:

- perturb role structure
- perturb reproduction channel
- perturb conflict suppression mechanism
- test for reversion under changed conditions

## 5. Required Metrics

To support the ladder, the project must later log and summarize:

### Group structure metrics

- `group_persistence_time`
- `group_member_turnover`
- `group_spatial_cohesion`
- `group_role_asymmetry`

### Group reproduction metrics

- `offspring_group_count`
- `offspring_group_survival`
- `group_lineage_depth`
- `group_founder_width`

### Fitness-decoupling metrics

- `counterfactual_solitary_fitness`
- `group_specific_fitness_advantage`
- `within_group_conflict`
- `dependence_index`

### Reversion metrics

- `group_collapse_rate`
- `loss_of_role_asymmetry`
- `return_to_solitary_viability`

## 6. Project Writing Rules

To keep claims honest, the project should use wording rules.

### Allowed early wording

- "stable group"
- "persistent cluster"
- "ecologically meaningful grouping"
- "candidate group-level effect"
- "candidate group-level selection"

### Restricted wording

Use only with explicit evidence bundle:

- "group reproduction"
- "higher-level selection"
- "fitness decoupling"
- "incipient higher-level individuality"

### Discouraged wording

Avoid unless Level 6 evidence exists:

- "a new individual evolved"
- "true multicellularity"
- "major transition achieved"

These phrases are too easy to overuse.

## 7. Failure Modes

### Failure mode 1. Clustering theater

Spatially tight groups look important, but do not reproduce or change fitness.

### Failure mode 2. Cooperation inflation

Resource sharing is interpreted as individuality without role dependence or
group-level inheritance.

### Failure mode 3. Reproduction inflation

Any group-associated birth event is treated as group reproduction even though
descendant structure is not group-borne.

### Failure mode 4. No reversion test

The organization looks strong only because it has never been stressed.

## 8. Bottom Line

The strongest lesson is:

```text
We should claim higher-level individuality only when groups begin to reproduce,
carry persistent lineage history, and reorganize fitness in ways that cannot be
explained by member-level success alone.
```

Everything short of that may still be scientifically interesting.
It just deserves a weaker name.
